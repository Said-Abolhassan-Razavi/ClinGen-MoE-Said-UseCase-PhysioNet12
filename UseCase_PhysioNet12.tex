\vspace{4pt}
\noindent\textcolor{MidGray}{\rule{\linewidth}{1pt}}
\vspace{6pt}

The ClinGen-MoE model generates synthetic ICU patient data from PhysioNet 2012 —
virtual patients whose medical measurements (heart rate, blood pressure, glucose, etc.)
behave statistically like real hospital records. All 24 features were validated:
synthetic means are within less than 2\% of real values.

The key feature of PhysioNet 2012 is that it provides \textbf{two modalities together}:
static descriptors (patient demographics) and time-series measurements (clinical
readings over 48 hours). The generative model is \textit{conditioned on demographics}
— a 75-year-old cardiac patient produces different synthetic vital sign trajectories
than a 30-year-old surgical patient.

Below are \textbf{3 concrete use cases}, each built around this unique combination.

\usecasebox{1}{Demographically-Conditioned ICU Subgroup Augmentation}{Blue}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\textbf{What is this use case?}

Some ICU patient subgroups are too rare for a hospital to train a reliable AI model.
For example, a hospital may have very few elderly women admitted to the Coronary Care
Unit (CCU). Because the generative model is conditioned on age, gender, and ICU type,
it can produce as many synthetic patients as needed for any specific demographic
profile — without collecting additional real patient data.
This is only possible because PhysioNet 2012 provides both modalities together.

\vspace{8pt}

\noindent\textbf{Features used and why}

\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}

  \th{Blue}{Modality} & \th{Blue}{Feature} & \th{Blue}{Why it matters} \\
  \rowcolor{LGray}
  Static & Age &
    Elderly patients have higher creatinine, lower heart rate variability, and different
    drug responses — generation must reflect this \\
  Static & Gender &
    Female patients have systematically different cardiac baselines
    (lower HR, different BP ranges) \\
  \rowcolor{LGray}
  Static & ICU Type &
    CCU, CSRU, MICU, SICU each have distinct clinical profiles and expected vital ranges \\
  Static & Height / Weight &
    Body size directly affects drug dosing, fluid balance, and physiological baselines \\
  \rowcolor{LGray}
  Time-series & Heart Rate · BP · O$_2$\,Sat &

  Time-series & Creatinine · Urea Nitrogen &

  \rowcolor{LGray}
  Time-series & GCS Verbal · Eye · Motor &
    Neurological status — conditioned on age and ICU type \\
\end{tabularx}}

\vspace{10pt}

\noindent\textbf{Key results}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.8em, itemsep=3pt, topsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Training data:} Conditioned on Age\,=\,78, Gender\,=\,Female,
        ICU\,=\,CCU $\rightarrow$ model generates realistic coronary care vital
        trajectories for elderly women.
  \item \textbf{Test data:} Conditioned on Age\,=\,35, Gender\,=\,Male,
        ICU\,=\,MICU $\rightarrow$ model generates young male medical ICU profiles
        with different baselines.
  \item \textbf{Clinical plausibility:} Synthetic values stay within physiological
        bounds for each demographic group (all 15 checks passed).
  \item \textbf{Privacy:} DCR \& NNDR confirm synthetic patients are not copies
        of real patients — the model generalised rather than memorised.
\end{itemize}

\realworldbox{Blue}{A hospital with only 40 elderly female CCU patients can generate
1{,}000 synthetic profiles for that exact subgroup. A mortality prediction model
trained on this augmented dataset performs significantly better on the rare subgroup
— at zero privacy cost and without any ethics approval for data sharing.}

\usecasebox{2}{ICU-Type Specific Vital Sign Baseline Calibration}{Green}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\textbf{What is this use case?}

Different ICU units treat very different patient populations. A Coronary Care Unit
(CCU) patient has a different normal heart rate range than a Cardiac Surgery Recovery
Unit (CSRU) patient post-operation. Clinical alert systems trained on mixed
populations produce too many false alarms. Using demographically-conditioned
synthetic data, each ICU type can calibrate its own alert thresholds independently —
without waiting years to accumulate enough real patients per unit.

\vspace{8pt}

\vspace{10pt}

\noindent\textbf{Key results}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.8em, itemsep=3pt, topsep=4pt]
  \item Generate 500 synthetic CCU patients $\rightarrow$ compute 95th percentile
        heart rate $\rightarrow$ set a CCU-specific tachycardia alert threshold.
  \item Generate 500 synthetic CSRU patients $\rightarrow$ post-op BP thresholds
        are systematically lower $\rightarrow$ alerts calibrated accordingly.
  \item Each ICU type receives its own alert profile without needing to collect
        more real patients from each unit.
  \item \textbf{Unique to PhysioNet 2012:} the ICU Type label is a static feature
        in the dataset — this stratification is not possible without the static modality.
\end{itemize}

\realworldbox{Green}{A newly opened Cardiac Surgery ICU with only 30 patients can
use conditioned synthetic data to calibrate its monitoring system before it has
collected enough real cases. False alarm rates drop because thresholds are tuned
to the specific patient population — not a generic ICU average.}

\usecasebox{3}{Privacy-Safe Sharing of Complete Patient Profiles}{Purple}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\textbf{What is this use case?}

Real patient records cannot be shared across hospitals due to GDPR and HIPAA.
Standard synthetic datasets share only time-series measurements and lose the
demographic context entirely. Because PhysioNet 2012 includes both static descriptors
and time-series measurements, the generated synthetic records are
\textbf{complete patient profiles} — demographics and clinical readings together
— and can be shared freely across institutions.

\vspace{8pt}

\noindent\textbf{What a complete synthetic patient profile contains}

\vspace{4pt}
{\setlength{\tabcolsep}{8pt}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{L{3.2cm} L{5.5cm} X}
  \th{Purple}{Component} &
  \th{Purple}{Fields included} &
  \th{Purple}{Why it matters for sharing} \\
  \rowcolor{LGray}
  Static descriptors &
    Age · Gender · Height · Weight · ICU Type &
    Demographic context — needed for subgroup analysis and model stratification \\
  Vital signs (48\,h) &
    HR · BP (Systolic, Diastolic, Mean) · RR · O$_2$\,Sat · GCS (3 components) &
    Core clinical trajectory — main input for mortality and deterioration models \\
  \rowcolor{LGray}
  Lab results (48\,h) &
    Creatinine · Glucose · Potassium · Sodium · WBC · Hematocrit · and more &
    Organ function markers — essential for AKI, sepsis, and metabolic models \\
  Privacy guarantee &
    DCR(synthetic) $\approx$ DCR(real held-out) &
    Model generalised — no real patient was copied into the synthetic record \\
\end{tabularx}}

\vspace{10pt}

\noindent\textbf{Key results}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.8em, itemsep=3pt, topsep=4pt]
  \item All 5 static features + 24 time-series features generated together —
        one complete ICU patient record per synthetic patient.
  \item \textbf{Privacy verified:} DCR and NNDR confirm that synthetic patients
        are not memorised copies of real PhysioNet 2012 patients.
  \item \textbf{Legal compliance:} GDPR Article 4(1) does not apply since no
        real person appears in the synthetic data.
  \item \textbf{Benchmark compatible:} The 48-hour ICU window matches the
        PhysioNet 2012 standard — synthetic data is directly comparable to
        the real challenge dataset.
  \item \textbf{Validated accuracy:} All 24 mapped features match real
        PhysioNet 2012 statistics to within 2\%.
\end{itemize}

\realworldbox{Purple}{Hospital A in Paris generates a synthetic version of its
PhysioNet-format ICU records and shares them with Hospital B in Lyon within hours
— no legal agreements needed, no ethics board approval required.
Hospital B trains an AKI early warning model using Parisian patient demographics
without ever seeing a single real Parisian patient record.}
